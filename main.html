Introduction

Our inspiration for this project came throughout our History classes about the Japanese Occupation, and we felt that we wanted people to understand Singapore’s rich history better. Thus, we created a project that aims to aid students in learning History - more specifically about the Japanese Occupation - while having fun and also instill students’ interest in the subject of History, and it comprises of a board game on the Japanese Occupation for Secondary One students, and we hope that this project will be beneficial towards aiding students in their development of historical knowledge.

Research

Literary Review
First, we will be analysing the practicality of board games in learning. Generally speaking, board games are shown to teach children number and shape recognition, grouping and counting, letter recognition and reading, visual perception and colour recognition, and hand-eye coordination and manual dexterity, all essential skills that children must learn. This shows that board games have the ability to teach other forms of skills too. [1][2]

Besides that, face-to-face interaction is inevitable while playing any board game. [3] This encourages discussion between players about the board game itself, and even some concepts that they learn from it.

Finally, a board game was designed to further students learning in the field of pediatric medicine. Players advanced by answering questions, which were crafted with emphasis in content from core pediatric textbooks correctly, correctly. A survey was conducted on 37 medical students and 12 residents who completed the board game to assess the extent to which they understand the purpose of the game. The survey responses suggest that they observed that the board game was a practical and engaging learning experience. [4] This seems to suggest that board game are effective in spreading knowledge and can be used for educational purposes. Several other sites also seem to agree with this idea. A numerical board game created for classroom teaching made the children engaged in it, and also show signs of improvement in numerical knowledge in the children. [5]

Now, we will be analysing the necessity of spreading historical understanding. Researchers in America once conducted a research on their students knowledge of History and they found out that most of the students lack historical knowledge. [6] They also found out that most American students have been lacking knowledge of History ever since 1943. [7] Other than that, researchers also found out that a lot of Singaporeans did not know about Operation Coldstore. They found out that only 16.65% of the 1500 citizens in the survey knew about Operation Coldstore, and 18.5% were aware of Operation Spectrum. [8]
